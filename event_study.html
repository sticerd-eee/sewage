5.1 Setting

This section is currently being revised as I read and process the recent DiD and event study literature. Some statements may be unclear or incorrect, which I will try to refine them as I go.

We can conceptualise the setting as an event study where sewage spills constitute the treatment. To reduce compute time, I have aggregated the data to the X-meter radius around the spill site level by taking the median of house prices.

  • Treatment is non-absorbing: spill sites can transition between treated and untreated states multiple times, as a location may experience large spill activity in one period and low in the next period (relative to other spill sites).
  • Treatment is staggered: spills occur at different times across different locations
  • Treatment measurement: spill severity is quantified by either incident count or duration in period \(t\) (e.g., monthly), which is naturally continuous. However, we can simplify this into a binary treatment by classifying houses within radius X of a spill site as “treated” when the spill severity exceeds either the mean or a specified percentile threshold relative to other sites.
  • Fixed effects: we can include both period and location-specific (spill site) fixed effects to estimate the effect of spills on house prices through within-spill site variation over time

Given the potential problems with the vanilla TWFE presented in the recent literature, I decide to use the Chaisemartin & d’Haultefoeuille (2024) estimator using the package DIDmultiplegtDYN. This estimator allows for a wide range of settings, including non-absorbing treatment, staggered treatment, continuous treatment, and many other settings that I still need to understand.

5.2 Basic Event Study Regressions

The following figures show the dynamic effect of sewage spills on house prices over time. Each graph plots changes in median house prices within X-metre radius buffers around spill sites, measured monthly. House prices are aggregated at the spill level using median values for each buffer distance. A buffer is considered “treated” in a given month when the number of spills exceeds either the average or a specified percentile of spill counts across all buffers, calculated over either a monthly or yearly period.

In the event study regressions, the effects are estimated for:

  • Same switchers only. This ensures that event-study effects are estimated only using switchers for whom all the event-study effects can be estimated. This avoids the problem that if treatment effects are heterogeneous across spill sites where treatment switches, changes in the estimated effects across different time horizons might reflect changes in the composition of the sample rather than actual dynamic treatment effects.
  • Normalised. This scales the estimates to represent effects per unit of treatment rather than total effects. For spill sites, this means showing the impact per unit of treatment intensity, making estimates comparable across time periods and sites.

Overall, all specifications show practically no effect of spills on house prices.
