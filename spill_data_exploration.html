3.1 What Is a Combined Sewer Overflow and How Does It Work?

A Combined Sewer Overflow (CSO) is a relief point built into older sewerage systems, a legacy of Victorian engineering, where a single pipe carries both household sewage and rainwater runoff. During periods of heavy rainfall, the volume of this mixed wastewater can overwhelm the network’s capacity. To prevent sewage from backing up into streets and homes, the CSO acts as an emergency safety valve. Once the flow reaches a specific level, it overtops an internal weir and is discharged, untreated, directly into a nearby river, lake, or the sea. While designed for use only in exceptional storm conditions, the system’s function is to prioritise the protection of property from flooding over preventing waterway pollution.

3.3 Geographic Distribution in 2023

3.3.1 Total Spills

The maps below display the total number and duration of sewage overflow spills, aggregated at the Middle Layer Super Output Area (MSOA)—a standard statistical geography in England designed to represent areas with roughly similar population sizes (~8000 people or ~3000 households).

Because MSOAs differ considerably in land area, it’s difficult to draw conclusions. Even so, sewage spills appear relatively evenly distributed across England. Fewer spill sites are visible around London, though this likely reflects the very small geographic size of MSOAs in high-density urban areas rather than a true absence of spills. There also appears to be heavier spill activitiy in the North and South West.

3.4 Frequency and Duration of Sewage Spills

Both the summary tables and the histograms show that the frequency and duration are very positively skewed. A priori, this could be due various factors (also considering that left side is bound at zero):

  1. Malfunctioning of EDM sensors: Malfunctioning EDM sensors may detect ‘blank’ sewage overflows, creating outliers in the right tail.
  2. Extreme weather events: Sewage overflows are (theoretically) caused by rainfall. Episodic heavy rain could create temporal clusters of long-duration events, stretching the right tail.
  3. Aging or Inconsistent Infrastructure: Older sewer systems or uneven maintenance across regions could lead to chronic sewage spills, stretching the right tail
